Welcome

Fish are among the oldest and most diverse vertebrates. They inhabit every type of aquatic environment—from alpine streams to the deepest ocean trenches—and have evolved an astonishing array of adaptations. This site collects some of the most remarkable facts about fish, from record‑breaking giants and minuscule minnows to strange behaviours and survival strategies that defy expectations.

Interesting Fish Facts

Species abundance

Scientists have identified more than 32 000 fish species—more than all other vertebrates combined—and new species are discovered every year[2].

Largest fish

The whale shark holds the record for the world’s largest fish. It grows over 50 feet long and weighs several tons. Despite its size, it has more than 4 000 teeth—each only 3 mm long[1][2].

Smallest fish

The world’s smallest fish is the tiny goby of the Philippines. Adults rarely grow longer than half an inch[1].

Most common fish

Deepwater fish called bristlemouths are thought to be the most numerous fish in the world. They are about the size of a small minnow and live at depths of around 500 metres or more[1].

Speed champions

Sailfish are among the fastest fish, able to swim as quickly as a car travelling along a highway[2]. Other speedsters such as tuna and certain sharks can sustain 5–10 miles per hour in long‑distance swims[1].

Dangerous beauty

The stonefish is the most poisonous fish in the world. Its venomous spines can cause shock, paralysis or even death if not treated promptly[2].

Born alive or from eggs

While most fish reproduce by laying eggs, some species—including the great white shark—give birth to live young called pups[2].

Land‑dwelling fish

Lungfish can survive out of water for years by secreting a mucus cocoon, burrowing into mud and breathing air with a lung as well as gills[2].

Longevity

The orange roughy can live for more than 100 years, and the Acadian redfish can live past 40 years[1]. Lifespans vary widely among fish.

Breathing underwater

Fish don’t breathe air; they extract dissolved oxygen from water. Their gills contain a network of fine blood vessels that allow oxygen to diffuse into the bloodstream[1].

Do fish sleep?

Most fish do not have eyelids. Instead of deep sleep, they enter periods of rest—floating motionless, wedging themselves into crevices or even building nests—while remaining alert for danger[1].

Swimming style

Fish swim by contracting bands of muscle on opposite sides of the body. This whips the tail back and forth. Vertical fins provide stability and paired fins help hover or steer. A handful of species, including seahorses and shrimpfish, swim upright[1].

No swim bladder

Sharks and rays lack a swim bladder—the gas‑filled organ that helps most bony fish stay buoyant. As a result, they must keep swimming or they will sink[2].

Underwater voices

Fish communicate using low‑pitched sounds such as grunts, croaks and hisses. They make noises by vibrating muscles against their swim bladder because they lack vocal cords[2].

School rules

Fish can form schools containing millions of individuals. A special row of pores called the lateral line runs along their sides and senses pressure changes, helping them maintain formation[2].

Amazing taste buds

Catfish have more than 27 000 taste buds spread over their bodies—nearly four times the number humans have[2].

Walking fish

Mudskippers spend much of their time on land. They “walk” on their fins and carry water in their gill chambers. They can also breathe through the pores of their moist skin[2].

Piranha origins

The word “piranha” comes from the Tupi term pira nya, meaning “scissors.” These fish have razor‑sharp teeth and feed on fish, insects, seeds, fruit and occasionally carcasses. Despite their reputation, there are no proven reports of piranhas killing people[2].

Epic migrations

Salmon undertake astonishing journeys. Tagged Chinook salmon in the Yukon River were documented travelling nearly 2 000 miles in just 60 days to return to the river where they were born[2].

Mysteries below

Humans have explored only about 1 % of the ocean depths. Scientists believe millions of species—including countless kinds of fish—remain undiscovered in the deep sea[2].
